The counterexample is a two-level graph: take a triangle-free "core" H on m vertices that is connected, has maximum degree at most d, and — crucially — has small independence number, α(H) = O(m·log d/d) (such graphs exist by a first-moment argument on sparse random graphs), and then attach s pendant leaves to every core vertex, with s ≫ 1/η. Almost all of the n vertices are pendants.

Now suppose G′ ⊇ G is triangle-free with diameter at most 4, and look at the pendants that G′ leaves "untouched" — more precisely, the components of the new pendant–pendant edge graph that receive no new edge to the core. By a spanning-forest count, the number of added edges is at least (number of pendants) − (number of such core-free components), so the whole problem is to show there are few of them. Any two core-free components must, by tracing a path of length ≤ 4 between them (which is forced to exit and enter through the original root edges), have roots at distance ≤ 2 in G′. And here triangle-freeness bites: the neighborhood of any vertex of G′ is an independent set, hence its intersection with the core is independent *in H*, hence has size at most α(H). So each added edge can bring only O(α(H)) = O(m·log d/d) core pairs within distance 2, and with fewer than n added edges the total number of close core pairs is K = O(m²·log d/d) — a vanishing fraction of all pairs. Since the ~N² pairs of core-free components must all be chargeable to these K close pairs (or to shared roots), N² ≲ s²·K, giving N = O(s·m·√(log d/d)). Choosing d large depending on s and η makes N ≤ η·n/4, so at least (1−η)n edges are needed.

In other words: the only way to bring all the pendants close together is to route them through a small "hub" structure, but in a triangle-free graph every hub's core-neighborhood must be an independent set of H, and H was built to have no large independent sets. So hubs have tiny capacity, nearly every pendant must buy its own new edge, and h₄(G) ≥ (1−η)n for every η > 0 — refuting the conjectured h₄(G) ≤ (1−ε)n. (This is tight: connecting all pendants to a single new hub vertex's worth of structure achieves diameter 4 with about n−m ≈ (1−1/(s+1))n edges, which is why the answer sits at exactly (1−o(1))n and not beyond.)
